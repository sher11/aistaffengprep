Module 6: Influence Without Authority

Learning Objectives

  • Demonstrate staff-level influence patterns
  • Show cross-team alignment abilities
  • Navigate organizational complexity
  • Lead without formal authority

Why Influence Matters at Staff Level

Staff engineers must:

  • Drive technical initiatives across teams
  • Change entrenched behaviors/practices
  • Align stakeholders with different incentives
  • Lead without formal reporting structure

Common Questions

Focus on: understanding stakeholders, building relationships, data-driven persuasion

"Tell me about leading a cross-team initiative"

Focus on: alignment challenges, coalition building, delivering results

Focus on: understanding their position, finding common ground, win-win outcome

Focus on: assessing morale, specific actions, team feedback

Staff-Level Signals

Phrases That Show Influence Skills ▼
  • "I didn't have authority, so I..."
  • "I scheduled 1:1s with key stakeholders to understand..."
  • "I presented data showing..."
  • "By finding common ground, I was able to..."
  • "I built a coalition with Teams A and B first..."
  • "I incorporated their concerns into my proposal..."

Example Response

Strong Influence Story

Situation: "We needed to adopt a new testing framework across three teams, but each team lead had their own preferred approach. I didn't have authority to mandate a choice."

Building Influence: "I scheduled 1:1s with each lead to understand their priorities. Team A valued speed, Team B valued reliability, Team C worried about migration cost. I created a comparison matrix showing how my recommendation addressed each concern. I found that Teams A and C had similar underlying needs, so I aligned them first."

Resolution: "With two of three teams on board, I scheduled a joint meeting. Team B raised valid edge case concerns, which I incorporated into the adoption plan. We agreed on a phased rollout."

Outcome: "Six months later, all three teams use the framework. Test coverage increased 40%. The relationships I built have made future cross-team work easier."

Anti-Patterns

Using Authority You Don't Have

"I told them we were doing it my way" shows poor influence skills.

Escalating First

Running to management before trying to build alignment.

Ignoring Concerns

Bulldozing over valid objections damages relationships.

Win-Lose Outcomes

Good influence finds solutions that work for everyone.

Influencing Upward: Persuading Executives and Senior Leaders

Why This Is Critical at Staff Level

Staff engineers frequently need to:

  • Get investment in technical initiatives (infrastructure, debt reduction, platform work)
  • Change organizational direction or priorities
  • Push back on unrealistic timelines or scope
  • Advocate for technical standards and practices

This requires different skills than influencing peers.

Worked Example: Getting Investment in Platform Work ▼

Scenario: You believe the company needs to invest a quarter in CI/CD improvements, but leadership wants all hands on feature work.

Weak Approach

"Our CI/CD is outdated. We're using old versions of Jenkins, our test suite takes 45 minutes, and we don't have proper staging environments. The engineering team is frustrated and we need to modernize our tooling."

Problem: Sounds like technical preference, not business case. No clear ask.

Strong Approach

Frame the problem in business terms: "Our deployment process is limiting how fast we can ship to customers. We deploy twice a week; competitors deploy daily. Each deployment requires 4 hours of engineer time for manual verification."

Quantify the impact:

  • "Engineering time: 8 hours/week = 400 hours/year on deployment overhead"
  • "Feature velocity: We could ship bug fixes same-day instead of waiting for weekly deployment"
  • "Incident recovery: Rollback currently takes 2 hours; could be 5 minutes"

Present options:

  • Option A (Recommended): 2 engineers for 10 weeks. Full automation. ROI positive in 6 months.
  • Option B: 1 engineer for 16 weeks. Partial automation. Slower but less impact on feature work.
  • Option C: Continue current state. Cost: 400 engineer hours/year + competitive disadvantage.

Make the ask: "I'm asking for approval to pull 2 engineers from feature work for Q2. We'll deliver automated deployments by end of quarter and measure impact in Q3."

Influence Story: Changing Organizational Direction ▼

Question fit: "Tell me about a time you influenced a decision at the organizational level"

Situation: "Our company was planning to build a custom analytics platform. Leadership had already committed budget and timeline to the board."

Task: "I believed buying a solution would be faster and cheaper, but the decision seemed final. I needed to reopen the conversation without being seen as undermining leadership."

Action:

  • "First, I validated my assumptions by talking to 5 companies who had made both choices - build and buy"
  • "Compiled data on total cost of ownership: vendor solutions vs. our estimate for custom build"
  • "Found that our build estimate didn't include ongoing maintenance - added 40% to true cost"
  • "Requested a 1:1 with the VP of Engineering to share findings. Framed as 'I want to help us succeed, and I'm concerned about some gaps in our analysis'"
  • "Presented not as 'you're wrong' but as 'here's new information that might change the calculus'"
  • "Offered to lead a formal evaluation process if leadership wanted to revisit the decision"

Result: "The VP appreciated the analysis and agreed to a 2-week evaluation. We ultimately chose a vendor solution that saved an estimated $1.2M over 3 years. I was asked to lead vendor evaluations for future build-vs-buy decisions."

Key Techniques

  • Gathered external data: Not just internal opinion
  • Private channel first: Didn't embarrass leadership publicly
  • Framed as new information: Not "you're wrong"
  • Offered to help: Not just criticism

Influence in Crisis Situations

Leading Without Authority During Incidents ▼

Crises often require rapid influence across organizational boundaries. Formal authority disappears - whoever can lead effectively, leads.

Crisis Influence Tactics

Tactic When to Use Example
Take command explicitly When no one is coordinating "I'll run incident command. Sarah, can you own customer comms? Mike, focus on database investigation."
Create structure When response is chaotic "Let's do status updates every 15 minutes in this channel. I'll keep a running timeline doc."
Summarize and focus When discussion is scattered "So we have three hypotheses. Let's assign one person to each and reconvene in 10 minutes."
Shield the team When external pressure is distracting "I'll handle stakeholder questions. Everyone else focus on diagnosis."
Make decisions fast When analysis paralysis sets in "We don't have perfect information. Let's try rolling back the last deploy - we can always roll forward."

Crisis Influence Story Example

Situation: "At 11 PM, our payment system started rejecting all credit cards. Multiple teams were investigating simultaneously but nobody was coordinating. Customer support was overwhelmed."

Task: "I was the most senior engineer online but had no formal incident commander role. The actual on-call engineer was junior and clearly overwhelmed."

Action:

  • "Announced in the incident channel: 'I'm going to coordinate unless someone objects. Alex, continue your investigation. I'll handle everything else.'"
  • "Created a shared doc with current status, hypotheses, and assigned owners"
  • "Established 10-minute check-ins and a separate channel for stakeholder updates"
  • "When we identified the cause (third-party API rate limiting), I made the call to switch to backup processor"
  • "After resolution, debriefed with Alex about incident management and offered to mentor"

Result: "Payment processing restored in 35 minutes. Post-incident review praised the coordination. I was later asked to formalize our incident command process and train other senior engineers."

When and How to Escalate Appropriately

The Escalation Decision Framework ▼

Escalation is a tool, not a failure. Staff engineers need judgment about when to use it.

Escalate When:

  • You've genuinely tried: Multiple attempts at direct resolution documented
  • Impact exceeds your scope: The decision affects areas beyond your authority
  • Time is critical: Delay would cause significant harm
  • Safety/compliance is at risk: Never delay on these
  • You need air cover: The decision needs organizational backing

Don't Escalate When:

  • It's just uncomfortable: Difficult conversations aren't escalation-worthy
  • You haven't tried direct resolution: Escalating first damages relationships
  • You want to "win": Escalation shouldn't be a weapon
  • The issue is trivial: Pick your battles

How to Escalate Effectively

  1. Document attempts: "I've had 3 conversations over 2 weeks and we remain stuck on X"
  2. Be fair to all parties: Represent the other perspective accurately
  3. State impact clearly: "This is blocking Y, which puts Z at risk"
  4. Propose solutions: "I see two paths forward: A or B"
  5. Ask for specific help: "Can you help us reach alignment on the priority?"

Sample Escalation Message

Subject: Need help aligning with Platform team on API migration timeline

Hi [Manager],

I need your help resolving a priority conflict with the Platform team.

Situation: We need their API migration complete by March 1 to meet our Q1 launch commitment. They've indicated they can't prioritize it until Q2.

What I've tried:

  • Met with their tech lead twice (Jan 5, Jan 12)
  • Offered to loan an engineer to help
  • Proposed reduced scope (they declined)

Impact: Without resolution, our Q1 launch slips to April, affecting $500K projected revenue.

Options:

  1. Leadership aligns on Q1 priority for both teams
  2. We build a workaround (adds 3 weeks but removes dependency)
  3. We accept Q2 launch

My recommendation: Option 1 if achievable; Option 2 as fallback.

Ask: Can you discuss priority alignment with Platform's director?

Building Influence Through Reputation and Mentorship

Long-Term Influence Building ▼

Short-term influence tactics work for specific situations. Long-term influence comes from reputation. Staff engineers build influence through consistent patterns.

Reputation Building Behaviors

Behavior Why It Builds Influence
Follow through consistently People trust those who do what they say they'll do
Share credit generously Others want to work with you and support your initiatives
Admit mistakes openly Builds trust - people believe you when you're confident
Help others succeed Creates allies who reciprocate when you need support
Communicate proactively No one is surprised - builds confidence in your judgment
Take on hard problems Demonstrates competence and commitment
Stay calm under pressure People look to you in crises

Influence Through Mentorship

Mentoring multiplies your influence. Engineers you've helped become advocates for your ideas and approaches.

  • Direct mentorship: Your mentees spread your technical philosophy and practices
  • Teaching: Running workshops or writing docs establishes expertise
  • Code review patterns: Your feedback shapes how teams think about code
  • Design review participation: Regular input on designs spreads your architectural thinking

Interview Story: Building Influence Through Mentorship

Situation: "I wanted to improve testing practices across the organization, but mandating standards through policy wasn't working - teams were resistant."

Task: "I needed to shift the engineering culture toward better testing without having authority over other teams."

Action:

  • "Started mentoring 3 mid-level engineers from different teams on testing practices"
  • "Ran a monthly 'testing patterns' lunch-and-learn open to anyone"
  • "Created example repositories showing testing approaches for common scenarios"
  • "Offered to pair with anyone struggling with testing during code review"
  • "Celebrated when teams improved their testing publicly in engineering all-hands"

Result: "Within a year, test coverage org-wide improved from 45% to 72%. More importantly, engineers started coming to me asking how to test things rather than asking if they could skip tests. Three of my mentees became testing advocates on their teams."

Advanced Scenarios: Complex Influence Situations

Multi-Year Initiative Influence ▼

Large initiatives require sustained influence over months or years. The techniques are different from one-time persuasion.

Long-Term Influence Strategies

  • Create visible momentum: Small wins build credibility and attract supporters
  • Build a coalition gradually: Start with early adopters, use success to convince skeptics
  • Document and share progress: Regular updates keep stakeholders engaged
  • Adapt to feedback: Incorporate concerns to broaden support
  • Be patient but persistent: Some battles are won over quarters, not weeks

Sample Story: Driving Org-Wide Adoption

Situation: "I believed our organization needed to adopt infrastructure-as-code, but there was significant resistance - 'we've always done it manually.'"

Year 1 - Prove value:

  • Implemented IaC for my team's infrastructure without asking permission
  • Documented time savings: 4 hours to spin up environment vs. 2 days
  • Invited curious engineers from other teams to our demos

Year 2 - Expand coalition:

  • Two other teams adopted our approach
  • Created shared modules that made adoption easier
  • Presented case study to engineering leadership

Year 3 - Organizational standard:

  • Leadership mandated IaC for new projects
  • I led working group to create org-wide standards and training

Result: "Three years after I started, 100% of new infrastructure uses IaC. Provisioning time dropped 90%. I never had authority to mandate anything - I just made it so obviously better that adoption became inevitable."

Leading Through Organizational Change ▼

Reorgs, layoffs, and leadership changes create influence vacuums. Staff engineers who step up during these times build lasting credibility.

Influence During Uncertainty

  • Be a source of stability: Consistent, calm presence helps teams navigate change
  • Fill communication gaps: When leadership is distracted, keep teams informed
  • Protect important work: Ensure critical projects don't fall through cracks
  • Avoid politics: Stay neutral during leadership transitions
  • Support new leaders: Help incoming managers succeed rather than testing them

Sample Story: Leading Through Reorg

Situation: "Our engineering org went through a major restructure. Teams were merged, managers changed, and there was a lot of uncertainty. Our critical Q4 launch was at risk."

Task: "I wasn't in leadership, but I was the most experienced engineer who understood all the systems involved. People were looking to me for direction."

Action:

  • "Created a 'launch readiness' doc listing all tasks and owners - maintained it as the single source of truth"
  • "Ran daily standups for the launch team across the newly merged groups"
  • "When people asked me political questions ('Who's really in charge?'), I redirected to technical work"
  • "Proactively briefed the new director on project status and risks - helped them look informed"
  • "Connected people across newly merged teams who needed to collaborate but didn't know each other"

Result: "We launched on time. The new director later told me that my steadiness during the transition was why she trusted me to lead future cross-team initiatives. Several team members thanked me for keeping things on track when everything else was chaotic."

Interview Tip

When telling stories about organizational change, focus on your stabilizing influence, not complaints about the change itself. Never badmouth leadership - even past leadership at previous companies.
